01 · DetectionWhere and when hazards strike

A weighted, spatially-clustered detection engine flags flash-flood and heatwave events across the Saudi grid. Validated against known 2025 events and a spatial-climatology check.

Annual hazard hotspots (2025). Flash-flood risk concentrates over the south-western Asir mountains and Red Sea coast; heatwave risk over the low, hot interior — the mountains stay cool. The pattern emerges from the data alone and matches Saudi Arabia's real hazard geography.
Known 2025 extreme events. The engine reproduces the 23 Aug Jizan extreme rain (254.9 mm), the 19 Aug moisture surge, and the July–August interior heatwaves — red rings mark extreme-severity clusters.

02 · Knowledge GraphWhy hazards form

An interactive causal knowledge graph links indicators, hazards, driving mechanisms (Red Sea Trough, moisture transport, orographic lifting) and real events — with data-driven correlations that recover known physics (e.g. sea-surface temperature ↔ convective instability). 4 of 5 mechanisms are now grounded in verbatim-verified quotes from 6 real, peer-reviewed publications (e.g. de Vries et al. 2013, JGR-Atmospheres, on the Active Red Sea Trough) — not just domain assertions.

03 · ForecastOne day ahead, not just today

A gradient-boosted spatiotemporal model predicts tomorrow's hazard risk from today's indicators (train on Jan–Jun 2025, test on unseen Jul–Dec) — genuine forecasting, not same-day detection. Verified with threshold-independent metrics, known-event checks, and negative controls; a spatial neighbour-context feature and a graph-neural-network variant were also tested and are reported honestly below.

One-day-ahead forecast vs actual outcome. Top row: predicted risk from the day before; bottom row: what actually happened. Heatwave forecasts (right two columns) show strong spatial correspondence. Flash-flood forecasting (left two columns) is harder — the 23 Aug Jizan hotspot is correctly located but under-confident, and the 19 Aug moisture-surge event is missed entirely, consistent with the rarity and localised, convective nature of flash floods.

  • Heatwave: strong one-day-ahead skill (ROC-AUC 0.971, PR-AUC 0.795 with a spatial-context feature) — calm days stay near-zero risk.
  • Flash flood: real but modest skill (ROC-AUC 0.873) given extreme rarity (0.5% of grid-days) and one year of training data; correctly ranks CAPE as the dominant driver.
  • Dust storm: real, out-of-sample-validated skill (ROC-AUC 0.887) for a similarly rare event (~2% of grid-days); the label itself was built from the detection engine's own already-validated rule rather than a separately invented definition.
  • Also tested, disclosed honestly: a spatiotemporal GNN improved flash-flood spatial recall on one event but degraded calibration overall — kept as a documented research direction, not deployed.
Reliability diagrams (is the probability itself trustworthy, not just its ranking?). Every point for all 3 hazards falls below the diagonal — the model is systematically overconfident: flash flood's "95.7%" bucket only sees the event 54.3% of the time; dust storm's "94.9%" bucket only 20.4%. Flash flood's aggregate ECE (0.006) looks best in isolation but is misleading — ~97% of samples sit in the near-zero bucket, masking the same overconfidence at the high end. Tested whether isotonic recalibration fixes this (leak-free 3-way split, model/10_calibration_fix.py): Brier score improved for all 3 hazards, but kept as a documented, tested finding rather than deployed to production — swapping calibrated probabilities would shift ~150 already-verified test/audit numbers and CAP's own severity thresholds, which are tuned to the raw scale. See agent/CALIBRATION_REPORT.md.

04 · Spatiotemporal ValidationReal vs. predicted, all 8 cities, across days

Per-city time series answer "was this city's forecast right on this day" — they don't show whether the risk field's shape across the map is right. These maps pair two independently-computed layers for 3 real, officially-reported 2025 events: REAL (the rule-engine ground-truth risk score, computed at every grid cell independently of the ML model) and PREDICTED (the trained model's forecast probability, evaluated at every grid cell rather than just the 8 city points — verified to reproduce the live agent's own forecast tool exactly, to 4 decimal places, across 10 spot checks before being trusted here).

Dust storm (Dammam), 16–19 May. REAL shows an orange/red band tracking the Gulf coast near Dammam on the 17th–18th, matching the city-level detection already reported. PREDICTED is broader and noisier — an honest difference, not a bug: the model's probability surface is continuous, the rule engine's is threshold-based.
Heatwave (Dammam), 11–14 June. This map surfaced a real inconsistency a single-city time series hid: REAL stays green/yellow at Dammam all 4 days (the rule engine requires tmax≥45°C locally; Dammam peaked at 44.2°C), while PREDICTED shows sustained high risk. The model's call was directionally right and matched the reported regional event (whose 49.3°C record was at Al-Ahsa, not one of this system's 8 cities) — but unlike the dust storm, the rule-engine ground-truth doesn't independently confirm it at Dammam's exact coordinates. Caught while building this map, disclosed here rather than smoothed over.
Flash flood (Jeddah), 8–11 Dec. Both layers stay green at Jeddah itself across all 4 days, consistent with the disclosed resolution limitation below. One caveat stated plainly: REAL shows a red cell on 9 Dec, but it sits ~165 km north of Jeddah and reflects a separate, genuine rain signal in the raw data (18 mm precipitation, CAPE 1700, IVT 339) — not the Jeddah event. Kept unedited so the map doesn't understate what the raw grid contains, but flagged so it isn't mistaken for confirmation at Jeddah.

05 · AgentAsk it a question

A DeepSeek function-calling agent wires the forecast model, the literature-grounded causal knowledge graph, and live conditions into one assistant. Every probability, mechanism and citation it states comes from an actual tool call — never invented. This is what makes the knowledge graph functional rather than just browsable: the agent queries it programmatically on every question, the same way a production detection system would.

See real worked examples →
  • Grounded, not generative. The system prompt forbids stating any number or citation without a tool result backing it — verified across 169 unit tests and 4 end-to-end test scenarios, including honest refusal on out-of-range dates and unknown cities.
  • Proven with a live A/B test, not just claimed. Running the same questions with the causal knowledge-graph tool removed showed 4/4 answers lost their mechanism/citation grounding — but crucially, 0/4 hallucinated a replacement; the agent honestly said it lacked a grounded explanation. Full transcripts: agent/ABLATION_REPORT.md.
  • Terrain-aware. Forecasts for mountain cities (Abha, Taif) are flagged as lower-confidence — a genuine model limitation (coarse feature grid understating steep local relief) found and disclosed while building this check, not assumed away.
  • Reflexive self-check. Every forecast is cross-validated against an independent, rule-based detection engine. This caught a real, disclosed model limitation: on 23 Aug (Jizan), the physical preconditions for flash flooding were already elevated the day before the storm, but the model gave only 12.5% — a genuine precursor signal it underweighted. See agent/REFLEXIVE_CHECK_REPORT.md.
  • 3 hazards, not 2. Dust storm was added with full depth — new indicators, a trained and out-of-sample-validated forecast model, and knowledge-graph grounding that reused an existing literature citation rather than inventing one. See agent/DUST_STORM_REPORT.md.
  • Self-audited KG utilization. Only 3 of 11 KG edge types were actually queried by any tool (~20% of edges) — found by our own audit, not an outside critic. A 5th tool, region_risk_tool, was added to use the previously-idle at_risk_of/exposed_to edges, raising usage to ~40% and surfacing a genuine pre-existing KG data-quality gap along the way. See agent/REGION_RISK_REPORT.md.
  • CAP 1.2 standards-compliant alerts (6th tool). cap_alert_tool converts a forecast into a real CAP 1.2 (Common Alerting Protocol, the OASIS standard MAZU's own national framework is built on) XML alert, ready for broadcast/siren/SMS infrastructure — severity from the detection engine's own thresholds, certainty from the reflexive check's model/rule-engine agreement, and status hardcoded to Exercise (never Actual) since this is a historical-dataset demo, not a live feed. See agent/CAP_REPORT.md.
  • WMO-standard verification metrics. Added POD/FAR/CSI/HSS (Probability of Detection, False Alarm Ratio, Critical Success Index, Heidke Skill Score) alongside ROC-AUC/PR-AUC, computed from the already-saved production models at each hazard's own operational threshold. Surfaces a real finding threshold-independent ROC-AUC alone can't: flash_flood has strong ROC-AUC (0.873) yet genuinely low POD (0.10) at its fixed threshold, since true flash-flood days are extremely rare — disclosed honestly, not hidden behind the more flattering number. See agent/METEOROLOGICAL_METRICS_REPORT.md.
  • Ensemble uncertainty — an honest answer to the overconfidence problem above, without touching the point estimate. Two fix attempts (isotonic recalibration, ensemble-averaging) both made the probability more honest but hurt POD/CSI/HSS — neither deployed. Instead, forecast_tool now returns a 4th field, uncertainty (mean/std/range across the same 5 independently-trained models), purely as added context alongside the unchanged production probability. Large disagreement among 5 otherwise-identical models is a genuine, per-query confidence signal, distinct from the reflexive rule-engine check and the threshold-based POD/FAR/CSI/HSS above — and since no decision-relevant number moves, nothing already verified changes. See agent/UNCERTAINTY_REPORT.md (and agent/ENSEMBLE_REPORT.md for the averaging attempt that was tested and rejected).
  • Literature evidence search (7th tool) — closing the citation-coverage gap. The formal KG has only 6 hand-verified citations (7 candidates were extracted; 1 was excluded after manual quality review — see the mid-term report §7); some city/hazard pairs (e.g. Jeddah/heatwave) have no grounded mechanism at all. Inspired by a real conversation about Relink (a query-driven evidence-graph technique) — whose actual reference implementation was read directly before adapting only its underlying idea, not its Neo4j+trained-ranker+LLM-predicted-relations stack, which this project's own anti-fabrication discipline argues against — literature_evidence_tool searches a 12-entry literature pool (7 extraction-corpus entries + 5 newly researched) via plain TF-IDF similarity, labeling every result verified: false until a human promotes it through the same verbatim-quote pipeline the original citations passed. See agent/LITERATURE_EVIDENCE_REPORT.md.
  • Static showcase, by design. GitHub Pages can't run the Python backend or safely hold an API key client-side, so the linked page shows real, verified agent transcripts with their exact tool-call traces rather than a live chat.
